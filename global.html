Q: How much plastic did our school use, what are the effects, and what can we do to minimize use?


Based on calculations and conservative estimates, we concluded that our school used about 3700 cups a day before the abrupt administrative seizure of plastic cups. This calculation was made using a t-interval and estimating that 3 jugs were used daily by the IHS population. But why does the usage of many plastic cups matter?

Plastic has a profound effect on the environment — plastic can severely pollute areas and release toxins that lead to gradual changes in gene expression in organisms. Our school contributes to this global ignorance of plastic disposal by not recycling plastic cups. Ithaca High School doesn’t currently recycle. The “recycling” bins are simply de facto trash cans.

A major consequence is the formation of an ocean of plastic. In oceans right now, there are millions of tons of plastic, even without considering other pollutants. This surfeit of plastic ruins habitats for marine life, and much of this plastic gets washed up on shore onto beaches and other coastline attractions. Frequently, we now see pictures of plastic in the stomachs of marine life or choking smaller animals in headlines, a future which could be avoided with the reduction in plastic consumption or with better care regarding plastic disposal.

Plastic also releases tremendous amounts of toxins as it decomposes. Plastic takes hundreds, or even thousands, of years to decompose, and toxins are constantly leaking out. These toxins find themselves in food and water of many organisms (including humans). Effectively, it means that we’re eating small amounts of plastic.

With a clear dystopia painted by the effects of improper plastic disposal, it’s clear that future action is what will allow us to circumvent such a future. How can we create a world without plastic? What singular steps can we take locally to contribute?

An alternative that our school can implement is to lower plastic use is to open the water fountains. This could eliminate the usage of all cups. It is known that the school is looking to implement clean drinking water facilities in the future, and that in itself could largely reduce plastic consumption by our student body.

Another alternative could be the usage of paper cups. Paper cups are biodegradable, environmentally friendly, and are relatively cheap. They’re widely implemented globally as well. It’s an existing solution that IHS could look to implement. However, certain paper cups come with plastic lining, which complicates the recycling process. The plastic lining makes them not viable to recycle, meaning that those models should be avoided.

A simple solution could be to recycle the cups rather than throw them in the trash. We are lucky in Tompkins County to have a recycling center, and the fact that IHS does not recycle is a shame, and an example of improper utilization of nearby resources. Those without recycling facilities near them would not be able to implement such an alternative, but it is a solution that our student body should push for.

When considering environmental issues, the overwhelming number of problems can seem insurmountable. Instead of focusing on an apocalyptic future, it is crucial to focus on smaller actions that we can take as individuals — any lasting change will be not the product of one large project, but dozens of smaller ones such as the recycling of plastic cups, cumulatively being the change for a better future.

But why do we need to consider alternatives to plastic storage?

This is because plastic has many harmful effects on the environment and animals. These can include:

Toxin Release Gene expression
Bisphenol A (BPA) and PS oligomer are both released as plastic decomposes. BPA and PS oligomer cause additional pollution; in addition, they can disrupt hormone function and can affect the reproductive system. These toxins can also disrupt the endocrine system. Plastic has been seen to drive animals away from the best adaptation. Relating to toxins, the toxins can be consumed by organisms (BPA is water soluble and can leech into food and drinks). This causes a disruption in the genes which can stay to the fourth generation, or even further! But how do the toxins affect the genes? This is still being discovered, but what is important is that plastic dissolving does affect gene expression.
